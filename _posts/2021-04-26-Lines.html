In Geometry, the notion of line or straight line was introduced by ancient mathematicians to represent straight objects (having no curve) with negligible width and depth. Lines are an idealization of such objects, which are often described in terms of two points or referred to using a single letter.

Vertical Lines

The X-intercept of a graph is the point where the graph crosses the X-axis (Horizontal Axis). A vertical line is a line, parallel to Y-axis and goes straight, up and down, in a coordinate plane. The slope of a true vertical line is always equal to 1. Think of a vertical line as being a wall in a house, it seems strong, but you can still go through it.

Horizontal Lines

The Y-intercept of a graph is the point where the graph crosses the Y-axis (Vertical Axis). Because functions must pass the Vertical Line Test, a function can have at most one Y-intercept. The Y-intercept is often referred to with just the Y-value of a graph. In the way of Graphic 1 below, the Y-intercept is equal to 2. Think of a horizontal line as being the ground you stand on, even though you can dig down and get much lower.

Slopes

Graphic 3: Here is an example of two Slopes!

The slope of a line, or sometimes referred to as the gradient in mathematics, is a number that measures the steepness and direction of a line, or a section of a line connecting two points. The slope is typically designated at the letter ‘M’. A line's steepness is measured by the absolute value of its slope, M.

If a line increases in height from left to right, it has a positive slope. If a line decreased from left to right, it has a negative slope.
