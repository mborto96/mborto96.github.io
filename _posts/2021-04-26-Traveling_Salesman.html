The Travelling Salesman Problem

The travelling salesman problem asks the following question: "Given a list of cities and the distances between each pair of cities, what is the shortest possible route that visits each city exactly once and returns to the origin city?"

Here is an example of the Travelling Salesman Problem. If the saleman started in Pittsburgh, Pennsylvania, which would be the shortest route to hit all cities and return to Pittsburgh?

Possible Path 1

The total distance here is 8,038 miles. This is the second best path!

The second to best path would be Pittsburgh to Los Angeles, then to Seattle, then Miami, and finally to Pittsburgh. The total length is 8,038 miles.

Possible Path 2

The total distance here is 10,983 miles. This is the worst path!

The worst path would be Pittsburgh to Los Angeles, then to Seattle, then Miami, and then to Pittsburgh. The total length is 10,983 miles.

Possible Path 3

The total distance here is 7,565 miles. Which is the best!

Due to how the cities are at the four regional corners of the United States, the best path, starting from Pittsburgh, would be to go to Seattle or Miami First, then Los Angeles, and then afterwards, visit the city that has not been visited yet, and then finally, return to Pittsburgh. The total mileage for the trip will be 7,565 miles.

The History of the Travelling Salesman Problem

The travelling salesman problem was mathematically formulated in the 1800s by the Irish mathematician W.R. Hamilton....

....and by the British mathematician Thomas Kirkman.
